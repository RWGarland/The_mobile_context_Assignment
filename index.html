History

The History of mobile phones

The history of mobile phones has come a long way since they launched.

The first mobile phone was created in 1973 by Motorola when they made a commercial handset known as the ‘Motorola Dynastic 8000X’, the first phone call was made to a rival phone company where one of the engineers Martin Cooper said that he was calling on a mobile telephone. This phone was known to have “30 minutes of talk-time, six hours standby” and had a price tag of around £2639(This wasn’t made with the mass market in mind). With its small amount of battery life and the high price tag it was the first step towards what we know today as the mobile phone (Goodwin, 2017).

Problems

The problems of touch screens

Touch screens have been created to react with the fingertips of a person but this will also mean that the screen is also going to react to the palm of the user, this clearly becomes more of an issue when the phone screen gets larger, the user will still have to reach to the top left of the screen to go to different pages when their palm will react to the screen. This issue is most prominent when it comes to the phones that now have a curved edge to it, like the Samsung Galaxy S7.

Further situations can arise from touch screens is “the lack of well-known standards in multi-touch interface design and in the use of gestures makes the user interface difficult to use and interpret.” (E.Motta, 2012) When it comes to users needing to use them, most multi-touch will rely on the use of two hands being used, there needs to be more ways to use the same functions with just one hand on the same device.

Changes

Changes over time

The changes can become a problem over time is that they won’t be consistent with how they were in the past, these changes can come from the updates that get downloaded on to the phones and tablets, the biggest change that became an issue is in the alterations on iPads. In the past dragging down from the top used to display notifications, it then got changed, now looks like the lock screen which has coursed some problems as people think they are locking their device, ”you'll find yourself on your lock screen, which Apple called a cover sheet during the betas. And this isn't a bug. This nuisance was intentional.” (Peterson, 2017).

A further problem that can come from the inconsistency of products, there are many different types of android devices out there, this mean that “there's a slight learning curve with each Android device” (Eadicicco, 2015) as many functions will be in different location on the screen as well as the phones behaving differently for usability with gestures and not all phones will be able to do the same things.

The iPhoneX

The extra gestures needed for the iPhoneX

Although the iPhoneX wasn’t the first smart phone to be completely bezel-less or have no home button but the iPhoneX is the best example to illustrate how gestures are the only way of using the phone other than the power and volume buttons. To use the phone a person is going to have to learn a lot of new gestures that are not normally used in most other phones. Instead of a home button they now have a bar at the bottom that needs to but swiped up to go to the home screen. There is also the extra gesture to get up the previously used apps where the user has to swipe up and over to the left or right to then switch between apps. At first using the phone this can be frustrating but once used to it and users find they are trying to swipe up on other devices. It has been said “This is the biggest learning curve of any iPhone ever” (Brownlee, 2017) and that’s because iPhones haven’t really changed until now, these extra gestures have to be learned.

The big differences

The diffrences of tablets to mobiles

Phones and tablets are not too dissimilar to each other however there are a few significant differences because there is more screen space that the user can work with, this means there are gestures that can be used using the whole hand; this is shown when the iPad user can either use 4-5 fingers to interact with the device.

A user can use 4-5 fingers to pinch the screen and this will act the same as using the home button on the device, this can make the whole process seem as if it is really smooth. The users will also be able to switch apps, as well as change between the previously used apps with ease.

Functionality

How people use their mobile phones

The way in which people use their mobile phones has completely changed since the inclusion of touch screen, the functionality of a mobile has changed, now phones can do so much more and they have become a lot more complicated from just a telephone to hold a convocation.

All these changes have happened rather rapidly and if users don’t keep up with the changes then they can feel as if they are being technically left behind. It has been shown that young people are more versatile to the new technology because they “are immersed in new technologies from birth and grow up with them as part of their every-day lives” (Healy, 2007), this means that using things with a touch screen are now imbedded in their nature, they can pick up a device and after a little play “a teen can figure it out without the book in under an hour” (Nickson, 2016) with no issues.

However it almost seems the opposite when it comes to the older generation, it almost seems as if they have to play catch up with how to use items like smart phones; “there is clearly a large cohort of people who feel excluded by technology. They find it a bit impenetrable" (Hosking, 2015). It also will be seen that they will be the ones to get frustrated with something not doing what they want only due to them not knowing how to do it, some said they “found it intimidating and complicated” which just adds to reason that they may struggle with the use of touch screens it has been shown that devises have a response time of 0.7 seconds but the elderly have a response time of about a second which means they might hit the screen harder than if it was a toddler using the device.
